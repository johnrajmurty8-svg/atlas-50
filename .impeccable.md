## Design Context

### Users

**The Mood-First Traveller** — someone planning a trip in the next 3–12 months who doesn't yet know where they want to go. They're not researching; they're *feeling their way* toward a destination. They arrive on a desktop browser, probably in an unhurried evening moment, with an open question rather than a search query. They have good aesthetic taste — they use Pinterest and Instagram for inspiration but find both structurally useless for making decisions.

The job to be done: **transform vague wanderlust into a specific, burning conviction** about one or two places worth going.

### Brand Personality

**Wandering · Sensory · Alive**

- *Wandering*: not purposeless — purposefully aimless. The globe invites drift. There's no right answer, no funnel, no conversion goal. The experience trusts the user to find their own path.
- *Sensory*: the interface should feel like it has texture. The warmth of cream on deep navy. The weight of DM Serif Display at 72px. The glow of a hotspot dot. Every detail should register as physical, not digital.
- *Alive*: the globe rotates without being asked. Hotspots pulse. The Moodboard breathes with parallax. This is not a static catalogue — it's a living atlas.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Ground truth:** The HTML prototype in `assets/protoype/` is the locked visual reference. Port it faithfully; don't stray.

**Tone:** Editorial magazine expressed as a cartographic object. Think: the inside of a well-worn Monocle issue left on a long-haul flight, crossed with the feeling of seeing a globe on a library shelf and wanting to spin it.

**Theme:** Permanently dark. `#050912` background — the colour of deep water at night. This is non-negotiable.

**Typography (locked):**
- `DM Serif Display` — all headlines, destination names, stat values, Moodboard hero title
- `Inter` — body copy, nav items, search input, introductory copy
- `JetBrains Mono` — all labels, chips, issue marks, region headers, bottom bar text

These fonts are locked from the prototype. Do not substitute or augment.

**Colour palette (locked):**

--color-ocean:          #070f1f;      /* Globe ocean, card backgrounds */
--color-cream:          #f4ecd4;      /* Primary text */

--color-cream-45:       rgba(255,220,170,0.45);
--color-cream-25:       rgba(255,220,170,0.25);

**Border radius: zero everywhere.** Sharp corners reinforce the print/cartographic aesthetic.

**Anti-reference:** TripAdvisor / Booking.com — ratings-driven, transactional, cluttered with CTAs, optimised for conversion rather than inspiration. If a design decision makes Atlas /50 feel more like a booking tool, reverse it.

Also avoid: glassmorphism as decoration, gradient text, rounded cards with drop shadows, the generic SaaS dashboard aesthetic (Inter + teal + rounded corners), anything that feels algorithmically generated rather than editorially curated.

### Emotional Arc

**Beauty → Scale → Conviction** — in that order.

1. **Beauty first**: the globe, the typography, the warmth of cream on navy — the experience itself should be rewarding before anything else. Users should feel "that was beautiful" within 10 seconds.
2. **Scale second**: as they spin the globe and browse, a sense of how big and varied the world is should grow. The 50 destinations are not a checklist — they're a reminder that the world is larger than remembered.
3. **Conviction third**: they should leave with 1–2 places burning in their mind. The filtering, the Moodboard, the Wishlist — all serve this final emotional beat: "I have to go there."

### Design Principles

1. **The globe is the hero.** Every layout decision defers to the Three.js canvas. Panels float over it; they don't compete with it. The globe should always feel like the primary experience.

2. **Restraint in decoration, specificity in detail.** No decorative flourishes that aren't in the prototype. But the details that ARE there — the cream hierarchy, the issue mark format, the `✦` wishlist indicator, the JetBrains Mono label tracking — must be precise and intentional.

3. **Editorial voice, not UI voice.** Copy reads like a magazine, not an app. "Fifty places, this season." not "View all destinations." "Your List" not "Saved Items." Every label should feel like it was written by an editor, not a product manager.

4. **Permanence over responsiveness.** Desktop-only in V1. Design for a fixed viewport (1440px reference) with absolute positioning. Do not introduce flexbox or grid flexibility that wasn't in the prototype.

5. **Dark by nature, warm by detail.** The background is near-black, but the cream tones and the single yellow accent prevent coldness. The palette should always feel warm, never sterile. Every new element should ask: does it read warm, or does it feel clinical?
